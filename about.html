    • Liu Kang, also known as Liu Kai, was born in Fujian, China in 1911. He is a pioneer oil painter who has established a local style in Singapore’s visual arts. He is also known as an artist because of his Balinese-themed figurative painting. Liu Kang is a well-known artist with a rich collection of works. But besides that, he also held various positions in the art school and influenced many of the next generation of artists and painters in Singapore.

    He is one of the most respected, influential and highest grossing Singaporean artists thus far and no doubt his works will continue to be appreciated for many years to come. He is also the father of the architect Liu Thai Ker.


    Early Life

    Liu Kang born in the year the Chinese Qing dynasty fell, he spent his early years in Malaysia. A mispronunciation by his principal at Muar Primary School led to his name changed from Liu Kai to Liu Kang. In 1926, he went to the Jinan University Middle school in China to continue his secondary school education. He also did take an art class during his school holiday at Shanghai college of fine arts (later known as Xinhua Art Academy).

    Liu Kang under the influence of Chinese artist and art teacher Liu Haisu (1896–1994), he admired, and often appropriated the styles of French-based modernist painters such as Paul Cézanne, Cézanne, van Gogh and Matisse.

    Liu Kang continued studied art in Shanghai and Paris (at the Academie de Grande Chaumiere from 1929 to 1933), and taught art in Shanghai during the 1930s.


    World War 2

    In 1937 Liu Kang moved to Muar when Japanese invaded China and then later came to Singapore. In 1942 when Japanese invaded Singapore, Liu Kang and his family fled back to Muar in Malaya. Therefore, several of his works is influence because of WWII occur.

    He witnessed many alleged atrocities by the Japanese soldiers on the local population, and after the war ended in 1946, he published a collection of sketches documenting these atrocities in a multi volume work titled ‘Chop Suey’. This series is first published in both English and Chinese, and in 1991 was translated into Japanese.


    Nanyang Style

    Nanyang style of painting was a creation and development of the mixture of western and Asian influences.

    Liu Kang was a founding member of the Singapore Art Society, and was credited with developing the Nanyang Style of painting, an art style associated with the Nanyang Academy of Fine Arts. His works are widely collected, with many of his works currently on display at the National Gallery in Singapore.

    In 1952, Liu Kang, Chen Chong Swee, Chen Wen Hsi and Cheong Soo Pieng went on a famous field trip to Bali in search of a visual expression that was Southeast Asian that also drove the development of the Nanyang style of painting.

    In 1970, Liu was awarded the Public Service Star by the Singapore Government. He was honored by the same agency in 1996 with the Meritorious Service Medal.

    Despite his failing eyesight and a failed cataract transplant in 1986, Liu Kang continued to paint and exhibit his works. His most significant exhibition prior to his passing was held in China in November 2000, after having postponed it for more than a decade due to the Tiananmen incident in 1989. Liu Kang artwork have been exhibited in Singapore, France, Hong Kong, Macau, Taiwan and the United States.

    In May 2003, the 92-year-old artist gave most of his paintings and sketches, amounting to over 1,000 pieces, to the Singapore Art Museum. He also unveiled a painting of three Balinese women, each carrying a basket, titled Offerings.

    He passed away on June 1st, 2004 at the age of 93. To commemorate the 100th year of Liu's birth, the National Art Gallery, Singapore, together with the Global Chinese Arts & Culture Society and Lianhe Zaobao, held a forum titled "Liu Kang: Tropical Vanguard" on 2 April 2011. The forum brought together a panel of established artists and scholars to discuss Liu's significant influence and contributions to Singapore's art history.
